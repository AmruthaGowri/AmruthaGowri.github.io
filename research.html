Research Direction

Machine Learning Security and AI Safety

My research explores how AI systems fail, how attackers exploit them, and how we can build trustworthy defenses.

Research Statement

I am interested in understanding how modern AI systems, especially large language models, can be manipulated, compromised, or exploited. My work combines empirical experimentation, interpretability, and secure system design to bridge the gap between theoretical vulnerabilities and real-world AI deployment.

My central question is: Can we trust models that we do not fully understand?

Research Themes

Backdoor Attacks

Studying hidden trigger-based malicious behaviors learned during model fine-tuning.

Mechanistic Interpretability

Analyzing activations, weights, layers, and circuits to understand where backdoors live.

Secure AI Deployment

Designing practical runtime defenses, guardrails, and auditing systems for real AI applications.

Questions I Explore

How do backdoors emerge during fine-tuning?

Are malicious behaviors memorized or learned as internal representations?

Can we detect hidden behaviors without training data?

How can lightweight defenses scale to large models?

What are the limits of interpretability in AI security?

How can AI systems be made safer for high-stakes deployment?

PhD Research Goals

  • Develop robust defenses against adversarial ML attacks.
  • Improve interpretability for security-critical systems.
  • Design secure architectures for LLM deployment.
  • Build practical AI supply-chain auditing tools.
  • Connect AI security research with real-world deployment needs.
